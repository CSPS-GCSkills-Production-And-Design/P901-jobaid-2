Assessment Methods

Choosing Assessment Methods and Tools

Key Points

  • Candidates must be assessed to determine if they meet each merit criteria. The essential qualifications must be met, at a minimum, for the person to be qualified.
  • The merit criteria can be assessed in any order.
  • Candidates can be eliminated as soon as it is determined they do not meet an essential qualification and any of the other merit criteria that may be established (i.e. asset qualifications, operational requirements or organizational needs).
  • The sub-delegated manager has the authority to use any assessment method they consider appropriate.
  • The use of more than one source of information can reduce personal bias and allow candidates that are more or less comfortable with a particular assessment method, to fully demonstrate their qualifications.
  • The assessment tools must be administered fairly, which does not necessarily mean using the same assessment tools for all candidates.
  • An assessment board may include multiple assessors for additional perspectives to support diversity and inclusion and manage personal bias. You may also have a single assessor if it makes sense given the type of process and the nature of the qualifications.
  • Choose and train assessment board members to ensure an impartial process.
  • Various people may be responsible for assessing the same merit criteria. Ensure there is consistency and that the information gathered can be combined to fairly determine the merit of each candidate assessed.
  • The sub-delegated manager retains the authority to make the hiring decisions. The assessment board is responsible for obtaining information on the candidate’s qualifications.
  • Linking expected answers or behavioural indicators of the qualifications to the work to be performed will allow you to fairly assess candidates against the job requirements and help avoid comparing candidates to each other.
  • In general, job-specific knowledge can be acquired once on the job, with the exception of expertise-driven professions. When designing knowledge assessment tools, it is important to only expect a knowledge level that is required on the first day of the job. Managers may design their own assessment tools or use existing tools, including standardized tests from the PSC Personnel Psychology Centre.

Education

Education refers to academic, vocational, or technical studies and training acknowledged through a credential or other official documentation from or accepted by a recognized Canadian educational institution.

Occupational Certification

The possession of, or eligibility for, credentials such as a license, certificate, registration, letter, paper or other documents that constitute acceptable evidence of occupational competencies. For example, Chartered Professional Accountant (CPA), Professional Engineer, Member of the Bar, Registered Nurse, Transport Canada or Canadian Coast Guard (CCG) Certificate of Competency for Navigation, etc.

Experience

Experience includes previous employment or professional skills a person has acquired through working or volunteering. The work experience sought is based on the immediate and future needs of the position, and on skills the appointee needs to accomplish the work and perform in the job.

Knowledge

What information the person needs to know to do the job.

Competencies

Competencies are generally used to express “characteristics of an individual which underlie performance or behaviour at work” that are required now or in the future. You can find examples of competencies here: Examples of competencies.

Core competencies: For performance management purposes, employees in the public service are evaluated based on the following four core competencies:

  • Demonstrating integrity and respect
  • Thinking things through
  • Working effectively with others
  • Showing initiative and being action-oriented

Although the global score generated by the performance management system does not apply to qualifications, the competencies may be appropriate, and the performance information may be useful in an assessment.

Functional competencies: are applicable to employees in a particular type of work, such as client service agents, financial specialists, program analysts or human resources professionals.

Technical competencies: are applicable to particular jobs.

Key leadership competency profile: make up the basis for selection (e.g. essential qualifications), learning and development, performance and talent management of executives and other senior leaders in the public service. You can find information on the key leadership competency profile here Key Leadership Competencies

Abilities

An ability is a natural talent or acquired proficiency that allows an individual to perform a particular job or task successfully now or in the future. It does not need to be associated with experience. Examples include:

  • Ability to work under pressure
  • Ability to communicate effectively orally
  • Ability to establish project priorities and meet deadlines

Personal Suitability

These are behaviours required to effectively perform the duties of a particular job, such as:

  • Discretion
  • Initiative
  • Flexibility
  • Effective interpersonal relations
  • Being a team player
  • Dependability

Application/Resume

  • Description
    • Typically includes information such as biographical data, education, occupational certification, achievements, experience, demonstrated skills and abilities, employment history, and volunteer activities. 
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Education, Occupation Certification, Experience
    • Pay attention to embellished experience; verify statements with applicant and/or references.
    • If foreign education, ensure that Canadian standards have been met (see Foreign Credential Recognition requirements).

Screening Questions and Cover Letter

  • Description
    • Questions related to merit criteria (e.g. education, experience etc.) may be included as part of the application process.
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Education, Occupation Certification, Experience, Knowledge, Competencies, Abilities, Personal Suitability
    • Cover letters or essay questions allow for a response in a longer format within a particular framework to respond to a specific topic or question.
    • Behaviour-based questions are common; for example, ”Explain where, when, and how frequently you had to do ‘X.’”
    • The use of multiple questions to assess a single qualification may also allow for a deeper response than a yes/no question will. (see Public Service Resourcing System Support Centre)

Work Samples

  • Description
    • Previous work that demonstrates the candidate’s abilities or achievements. Usually in writing but may be in other mediums such as drawings, videos, blogs, etc.
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Experience, Knowledge, Abilities, Personal Suitability
    • Can support information gathered from other methods.
    • Must determine the extent of the candidate’s actual contribution.
    • Take into account that the work and standards may have occurred in a different context.
    • Consider that not all candidates may have the authority to share work samples.
    • This assessment method is the best representation of work requirements, which further removes potential barriers based on the assessment itself. It also allows early dialogue on workplace accommodations
  • Resources
    • Reviewing past accomplishments and experience

Written Assessments

  • Description
    • Paper-and-pencil, online or computer-based tests consist of assessment tools in which questions are read by candidates and answered in writing.
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Experience, Knowledge, Abilities, Competencies, Personal Suitability
    • Permits uniform evaluation of a broad range of factors.
    • Efficient way to assess many candidates simultaneously or accommodate for specific functional needs.
    • Questions may include behaviour based, situational as well as short-answer, long-answer, multiple-choice and true/false.
    • May be a take-home test (unsupervised) or in-person (supervised) or online (unsupervised).
    • Recommend following up an unsupervised test with one that is supervised.
  • Resources
    • Paper-and-pencil instruments: An efficient method of assessment
    • Best practices for unsupervised testing
    • Testing in the Public Service of Canada

Candidate Achievement Record (CAR)

  • Description
    • A tool that assesses competencies through self-reported examples of past performance and work-related achievements. Candidates are provided a template to describe situations in which they have demonstrated behaviours associated with various competencies.
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Competencies
    • There is a formal CAR available from the Personal Psychology Centre to assess key leadership competencies.
    • This tool may be replicated in-house to assess departmental competencies including core competencies.
    • A departmental CAR may be best used for internal candidates versus external candidates.
    • Typically, the behavioural indicators are provided to the candidates and it is requested that they provide the name of a validator(s) for their examples.
  • Resources
    • The Candidate Achievement Record (CAR)

Structured Interview

Structured Interview

  • Description
    • Questions that are prepared in advance that are linked to the requirements of the position with established criteria and expected responses.
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Knowledge, Experience, Abilities, Competencies, Personal Suitability
    • Permits explanation, elaboration of specific examples and clarification of information gathered from other methods.
    • Opportunity to provide candidate information about the job and answer their questions, which may reduce drop-off rates of qualified persons.
    • Flexibilities in conducting in-person, by phone, virtually, and could be pre-recorded.
    • Types of questions usually include behavioural and/or situational.
    • Interviews may also take an unstructured format, however. This is best used for selection once candidates have been found to be qualified.
  • Resources
    • The Structured Selection Interview: A Sound Method of Assessment
    • Video Assessment: Video Recruitment Toolkit
    • Appointment processes: how to conduct interviews

Simulation and Role Play

  • Description
    • A practical demonstration of qualifications in a real-life situation either independently (simulation) or with interaction with one or more board members (role-play).
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Knowledge, Abilities, Competencies, Personal Suitability
    • Effective for evaluating qualifications in which the candidate may not have prior experience.
    • Allows for flexibility since the assessors can probe and gain clarification.
    • Consider the time and cost to develop and to assess, as they may be more complex depending on the qualification.
    • Simulations, or situational exercises, are designed to place candidates in realistic situations to lead them to demonstrate one or more qualities relevant to the position.
  • Resources
    • Developing Simulations

In-Basket Exercise and Case Study

  • Description
    • In-basket exercises include background information (e.g. an organization’s mission statement and organizational chart) that is provided to the candidate along with documents normally found in the in-basket of someone in the position, such as memos, letters, and reports. A case study usually provides all the details of an actual or fictional situation in which the candidate has to address specific questions.
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Knowledge, Abilities, Competencies, Personal Suitability
    • The PSC Personnel Psychology Centre has in-basket exercises that are standardized and can aid in selecting as well as identifying training and development needs.
    • You may create your own in-basket exercise or case study and incorporate it into the overall written assessment and/or interview portion of the assessment.
    • Permits the candidate to be evaluated based on a real-life situation.
  • Resources
    • Public Service Commission Tests

Group Assessment

  • Description
    • Candidates are grouped together and either interviewed simultaneously as they take turns responding to interview questions (group interview) or are given a limited amount of time to discuss or resolve an issue with no other instruction or intervention from the assessors observing them (leaderless group discussion).
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Abilities, Competencies, Personal Suitability
    • Group assessments are most useful when they are part of a multi-method assessment strategy, which provides a comprehensive and reliable understanding of candidates’ competencies.
    • May be beneficial to assess multiple candidates at the interview stage and when qualifications are difficult to assess using other methods.
    • A real-life situation (role-play) where several candidates take on different roles may also be an option.
    • Candidates appreciate the job-realistic approach.
  • Resources
    • Best Practices for Using Group Assessments for Selection

Reference Checks

  • Description
    • Reference checks may consist of contacting current and former managers, supervisors, colleagues and clients to clarify and complete information already collected. The intent is to obtain information directly from the persons who could provide insight into the candidate’s experience, ability, competency and personal suitability. 
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Experience, Abilities, Competencies, Personal Suitability
    • There are flexibilities in that checks may be conducted in person, by phone, in writing or as a recorded reference check.
    • The focus should be on observed behaviours rather than opinions or impressions.
    • Consider who may possess the information you are seeking – it may not be the candidate’s manager.
    • Are the examples, recent and relevant and is what they are telling you a ”one;off” or a consistent pattern of behaviour?
    • Often used only as a verification but this may be a rich source of information.
    • Consider the nature and relationship as well as the duration.
    • Limitations may exist for external applicants, candidates returning from leave, and referees failing or unable to provide information that may be relevant.
    • Full-circle reference checks or 360°-feedback are methods in which managers, clients, colleagues and subordinates are contacted to provide more inclusive reviews of the candidate’s qualifications.

    • Structured reference checks

Performance Reviews

  • Description
    • The formal evaluation of performance against work objectives and competencies (expected behaviours). This may include core competencies as well as technical and functional competencies.
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Experience, Abilities, Competencies, Personal Suitability
    • A direct link must be established between the skills assessed in the performance review and the qualifications for the position.
    • May be best when used in conjunction with other assessment methods for internal processes.
    • Consider the level of the substantive position and the department’s objectives and expected behaviours and results.
    • Although a candidate may excel in their current position, it does not necessarily mean they are qualified for the next position.
    • Consider that not all candidates have a current or recent evaluation available (e.g. external candidates, some federal departments, and both RCMP and Canadian Armed Forces members may be internal candidates that do not use the same systems as the broader public service).
  • Resources
    • Performance Reviews in the Appointment Process

Personal Knowledge

  • Description
    • The use of assessment board members’ and hiring manager’s firsthand observations or experience of the candidate that identifies demonstrated qualities and behaviours relevant to the qualifications assessed.
  • Highlights
    • Good to evaluate: Education, Experience, Knowledge, Abilities, Competencies, Personal Suitability
    • The fact-based information that is documented for the process and may include positive or negative that can support or negate information gathered from other tools.
    • Consider the frequency and context of the demonstration of the qualification (or failure to demonstrate). Ensure to seek out a balanced evaluation.
    • It is similar to a reference check and performance appraisal; you are encouraged to use all information available to obtain a well-rounded view of a candidate.
    • Avoid using opinions or general impressions and focus on the merit criteria.

Considerations

When choosing an assessment method, you may consider the following factors:

  • The cost, time and other resources needed for administration. The more urgent the staffing need, the more you may want to consider some methods and not others.
  • The importance of the qualification being assessed. If the qualification is important, it might be worthwhile to put in the time and resources required for a thorough assessment.
  • The type of qualification. Certain qualifications lend themselves more easily to certain tools.
  • The work context in which the qualification applies: Consider selecting an assessment tool that reflects the requirements of the position.
  • The number and location of candidates. Selection of an assessment method may depend on the anticipated number of candidates or the area of selection.
  • The advantages and shortcomings of various assessment methods.
  • The type of staffing process,g. advertised vs. non-advertised, may lead you to choose different methods of assessment.
  • Accessibility and inclusion, by using the Fairness Review Checklist developed by the Public Service Commission.  

Assessment Practices

  • Active assessment information refers to information obtained directly from candidates or other persons. It requires development and administration but can be tailored to meet specific needs. Consider if your own biases are leading you to seek information that corroborates your impressions, or if it validates the candidate’s actual qualifications.
  • Passive assessment information makes use of existing sources that were gathered and recorded for a different purpose such as degrees and certificates, letters of reference, samples or work, an assessment for an unrelated appointment process, or a performance assessment. This method may save time associated with development and administration of assessment tools and provide a valid source of assessment information, or information that can be easily validated from other sources.
  • Sources of information
    • Candidates provide information on their resumes or job applications, or through written tests, interviews and other means. Consider that many candidates are putting their best foot forward, which may lead to exaggeration and false claims.  Other candidates may under-rate their own contribution or provide vague generalities.  Consider how to clarify and supplement the candidate’s information by probing for specific details about their role or validating it by seeking information from other sources.
    • Other persons, such as supervisors, colleagues, clients and direct reports can provide you with information through written and verbal references. Each has their own bias, which may lead to more positive or negative conclusions than you would draw in the same circumstance. Consider probing for specific examples, examining the context of these remarks, and validating it by seeking information from other sources.
    • Direct personal knowledge refers to the situation where the sub-delegated manager or a member of the assessment board has direct knowledge of the candidate that could be used as a reference, in another circumstance. It is difficult to disregard personal knowledge, even if you recuse yourself from assessing this one candidate, so the key is to consider how it can be incorporated into the assessment. Make the links to the merit criteria and consider validating by seeking information from other sources.
  • What form is the information?
    • Verbal information is usually recorded by assessors taking notes but may also include audio and video recordings (with proper permission, classification and storage). The notes are used to assist in recall to complete the assessment.  This method allows for active probing and adaptation to the unique context of the candidate to ensure you gather information that is complete.  Consider pre-planning interview questions and assigning roles to the assessors to ensure that the requirement to record notes does not interfere with the dialogue.
    • Written information may be requested through the job application, written tests and reference questionnaires. Some statements in an email may be sufficient, such as an employment equity self-declaration.  Other times, a candidate showing that they meet a qualification may include documents such as diplomas or letters.
  • Assessment Accommodation
    • Assessment accommodation is a change made to an assessment procedure, format or content. It is designed to remove barriers for a fair assessment and allow candidates to fully demonstrate their competency.
    • The assessment may be modified to provide accommodation for any of the grounds of discrimination set out in the Canadian Human Rights Act. The candidate does not have to self-declare as a person with a disability to request an accommodation measure.
    • People have the right to request assessment accommodation based on any of the human rights grounds. The right to request it does not necessarily mean that candidates will be accommodated, however. Delegated managers have the responsibility to determine assessment accommodations based on the limitations of the candidate and the work requirements; the objective is to remove barriers and not to lift candidates from their obligation to meet merit requirements.
    • IMPORTANT Therefore, when considering accommodation measures, the changes to the assessment procedure or modifications to the content of the assessment should not alter the nature or level of the qualification being assessed.
    • Assessment accommodations are one important means to make the assessment process more accessible and inclusive. Other means include the development of tests and assessments methods which are universal by design. It also includes the development of merit criteria, which include many ways to achieve the work requirements. Inclusive and accessible assessment is also supported by the development of adapted formats. 
  • To learn more about accommodation and inclusive assessment in the hiring process, please consult:
